About the device

What it is for

The TinyML Smart Plug is an added protection layer for residential appliance use. It monitors voltage, current, socket temperature, and waveform behavior so it can warn or trip on conditions that ordinary breakers may not describe clearly, especially progressive or intermittent fault conditions.

It works as complementary protection. It does not replace proper wiring, proper breakers, or safe appliance selection.

How it decides

The device uses a hybrid method. Arc faults are evaluated by an AI model using waveform features. Overload, socket heating, short circuit, undervoltage, and overvoltage use direct threshold rules for faster and more predictable protection behavior.

Arc signatures used the AI model because they are complex, but for threshold-based faults, using AI may prove harmful instead.

Status banner

The large banner is the fastest summary of the current state. If fresh live data stops arriving for about 15 seconds, the dashboard changes to disconnected so stale values are not mistaken as current.

System overview

This summarizes overall health, connectivity, power condition, active protection state, the latest recorded event, and other quick health indicators.

Voltage, current, apparent power, and temperature come from the latest live packet. On-card hints are intentionally short because the detailed explanations live here in the guide.

Protection rules

Arc Fault

Trips when the AI model detects an arc signature from waveform features.

Overload

Alarm at 10 A or above.

Socket Heating

Trip at 70 °C or higher.

Short Circuit

Trip above 20 A.

Undervoltage

Trip below 200 V.

Overvoltage

Trip above 250 V.

Waveform feature guide

Cycle NMSE

Measures how different one electrical cycle is from the next. Strong arcs often reduce cycle-to-cycle repeatability.

Zero-Cross Variance

Tracks irregularity in zero-cross timing, which is useful because arcs disturb expected current behavior around crossing points.

Zero-Cross Dwell Ratio

Shows how long the waveform stays near the zero region. This helps reveal abnormal conduction around zero crossings.

Pulse Count per Cycle

Counts pulse-like activity within a cycle. Increased pulse behavior can appear during unstable conduction and arcing.

Peak Fluctuation CV

Describes how much waveform peaks vary from cycle to cycle. Large variability can indicate instability.

Midband Residual RMS

Measures residual waveform energy after removing smoother expected behavior, helping expose abnormal mid-frequency content.

HF Band Energy Ratio

Looks at the amount of higher-frequency content relative to the signal. Arcing often increases high-frequency energy.

Wavelet Packet Energy Entropy

Captures how spread and disordered the signal energy becomes across wavelet bands.

Spectral Entropy

Measures randomness in the frequency spectrum. More disorder often means a less stable waveform.

THD Current

Total harmonic distortion of current. This is a supporting distortion feature, not a complete arc decision by itself.

How to respond to each fault type

Arc Fault

Stop using the appliance immediately. Unplug it and inspect the plug, cord, socket, and load for burning smell, carbonization, loose blades, or intermittent contact.

  • Avoid repeated reconnection tests while the cause is still unknown.
  • Replace damaged plugs, loose sockets, or appliances with suspicious cords.

Overload

Reduce the load current before continuing use. Separate high-draw appliances and avoid stacking heaters, irons, kettles, or similar loads on the same protected outlet path.

  • Use the dashboard current reading to verify the load returns below the safe range.
  • Avoid undersized extension cords for heavy loads.

Socket Heating

Let the outlet and plug cool first. Check for loose contact, discoloration, melted plastic, oxidized plug blades, or poor socket grip.

  • Do not keep using a warm outlet just because the appliance still works.
  • Replace worn sockets and plugs before reuse.

Short Circuit

Do not reconnect the load until the cause is found. Inspect the appliance, cord, and wiring path for direct faults, liquid ingress, crushed insulation, or internal damage.

  • If the source is not obvious, stop and use qualified electrical inspection.
  • This is a high-risk fault and should not be treated as a nuisance trip.

Undervoltage

Wait for the mains supply to stabilize before resetting. Brownout conditions can stress motors, compressors, and power supplies.

  • Avoid repeated restarts during sagging mains.
  • Be especially cautious with refrigerators, pumps, and motor-driven loads.

Overvoltage

Keep sensitive electronics disconnected until mains returns to normal. Overvoltage can damage chargers, adapters, control boards, and power supplies very quickly.

  • Wait for measured voltage to normalize before reuse.
  • Consider surge protection or checking the supply source if it repeats.
General rule: never treat a protection trip as something to bypass. Remove the cause first, then reset only after you have confirmed the appliance and outlet are safe.

How to reset the device after it trips

  1. Stop using the connected appliance immediately.
  2. Read the fault shown on the dashboard so you know what condition was detected.
  3. Inspect the appliance, plug, socket, and nearby area for heat, damage, burning smell, moisture, loose contact, or abnormal wiring conditions.
  4. Correct or remove the suspected cause first. Do not reset first and inspect later.
  5. Only after you personally confirm the environment is safe, press the physical reset button on the device.
  6. Reconnect and watch the dashboard again. If the same trip returns, discontinue use and inspect the appliance or installation more thoroughly.
The relay cannot automatically or remotely reset. This is intentional. The user must physically confirm safety and then press the physical reset button on the device.
